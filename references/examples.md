# Cross-Scenario Examples

Use these examples to understand routing and output depth. Do not reuse their outlines mechanically.

## Company capability briefing for a CIO

**Input:** Company background, product portfolio, delivery cases, a 20-minute meeting, and a CIO audience.

**Plan:** Clarify the desired next step; extract the customer's strategic language; organize capabilities around the customer's priorities rather than internal product lines; balance platform architecture, governance, delivery, and outcomes; keep detailed product catalogs in the appendix.

**Core claim example:** The company can support a governed path from AI experimentation to reusable enterprise capability and scaled delivery.

## Customer project solution for EB and TB

**Input:** Historical project documents, customer requirements, meeting notes, and an existing proposal.

**Plan:** Build one intelligence brief and content master. Derive an EB version emphasizing urgency, value, risk, proof, and requested investment decision. Derive a TB version emphasizing standards, architecture, integration, security, validation, and technical boundaries. Preserve factual consistency and record the adaptation matrix.

## Product solution such as AI digital employees

**Input:** Product knowledge, market context, previous slides, scenarios, implementation method, and cases.

**Plan:** Establish why the problem requires a product or organizational change, define the product category, connect architecture to business scenarios, show an implementation path, and prove value. Treat the topic as one example of product solution planning, not as the default deck framework.

## Existing-deck optimization

**Input:** A PPTX and a request such as “make this suitable for a technical review.”

**Plan:** Diagnose the current decision goal, narrative, evidence, repetition, density, and audience fit. Classify the work as local refinement, reorder, rebuild, or audience split. Present the revised management view before detailed slide rewrites.
